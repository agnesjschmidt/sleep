Sleep Recovery Quality

Your best work starts
the night before.

Most founders track output. Hours worked. Tasks closed. Revenue moved. Almost none track the single variable that determines cognitive quality for everything else: how recovered you actually are when you sit down to work.

Sleep is not rest. It is active biological maintenance — memory consolidation, hormonal reset, cellular repair. Miss it once and you operate on deficit. Miss it consistently and you are making company-defining decisions with a systematically degraded brain, without knowing it.

This module does three things. It assesses your current sleep quality with precision — not vague questions about whether you feel tired, but the specific variables that predict cognitive performance. It gives you a targeted training protocol matched to your exact gaps. And it tracks your recovery trend over time so you can see whether interventions are actually working.

This is not about sleeping more. It's about recovering better. Elite operators — Navy SEALs, F1 drivers, top-tier surgeons — treat sleep as a performance input, not a luxury. You should too.

Training Protocols

Targeted Interventions

Select the protocol that matches your primary gap identified in the assessment. Each protocol is a step-by-step implementation — not general advice, but specific daily actions with timing, sequence, and rationale.

Protocol A

Sleep Architecture Repair — for founders waking multiple times, feeling unrefreshed, or scoring low on deep/REM indicators.

Target: Improve sleep continuity and slow-wave sleep quality. Duration: 14 days minimum.

1

Set a non-negotiable wake time

Pick one wake time and hold it for 14 days — including weekends. This is the anchor of your circadian rhythm. The body builds sleep pressure (adenosine) relative to wake time, not sleep time. A consistent wake time regulates everything downstream. Start here even if you go to bed late the night before.

Duration: Permanent anchor, effective from day 1
2

Cold-dark bedroom environment

Body temperature must drop 1–3°C to initiate and maintain deep sleep. Set room to 17–19°C. Block all light (blackout) — even small light sources suppress melatonin. This is the single highest-ROI physical change for sleep architecture.

Setup: Day 1–2
3

Eliminate alcohol within 3h of sleep

Alcohol is the most commonly overlooked sleep disruptor. It suppresses REM directly — even 1–2 drinks fragment architecture significantly. If you drink, finish at least 3 hours before bed. Track this as a binary variable for 14 days.

Immediate: Night 1 onward
4

Magnesium glycinate before bed

300–400mg magnesium glycinate, 30–60 min before sleep. Magnesium activates GABA receptors and suppresses cortisol. One of the few supplements with robust evidence for sleep quality improvement. Glycinate form is best tolerated. Do not use magnesium oxide (poor absorption).

Daily: 30–60 min pre-sleep
5

No eating within 2–3h of bed

Late eating elevates insulin and core body temperature, both of which interfere with deep sleep onset. Finish your last meal 2–3h before your target sleep time. If hunger is a problem, a small amount of protein (not carbs) is acceptable.

Daily discipline
6

Morning HRV or recovery check

Use a wearable (Oura, Garmin, Whoop) or a free app (HRV4Training) to measure HRV each morning. Track your trend over 14 days, not individual scores. A rising HRV trend is objective confirmation that your sleep architecture is improving. This is your progress signal.

Daily: Within 5 min of waking
Protocol B

Wind-Down System — for founders who can't fall asleep, take long to feel tired, or carry the day's activation into bed.

Target: Build a physiological transition from work-state to sleep-state. Duration: 7 days to establish, 21 to solidify.

1

Hard stop: work ends 90 minutes before bed

No work, no strategy, no Slack after your designated cutoff. Stress hormones (cortisol, norepinephrine) take 60–90 minutes to metabolize after activation. Working until bed means you are literally injecting stress hormones into your sleep-onset window. Set an alarm. Treat the cutoff as non-negotiable.

Daily: 90 min pre-sleep
2

Dim all lights from cutoff onward

Light above 10 lux in the 2 hours before sleep suppresses melatonin by up to 50%. Use only warm, dim lighting after your cutoff. If you use screens: blue-light glasses (not mode — physical glasses) or switch entirely to physical books. Candles are effective if practical.

Daily: From work cutoff
3

Body temperature drop: hot shower or bath

Counterintuitive but well-evidenced: a 10–15 min hot shower 60–90 min before sleep accelerates core body temperature drop post-shower, triggering sleep onset. The body's attempt to cool down after heat exposure directly induces sleepiness. This is one of the most effective single-action interventions.

Daily: 60–90 min pre-sleep
4

Physiological sigh (30 days, nightly)

Double inhale through the nose (short inhale → second quick inhale → long exhale through mouth). 5 repetitions. This activates the parasympathetic nervous system faster than any other breathwork technique. It is the fastest-acting physiological tool for state shift from activation to rest. Use it as you get into bed.

At bedtime: 2 min
5

Non-sleep deep rest (NSDR) if needed

If you still feel activated at bedtime: a 10–20 min NSDR (Yoga Nidra protocol) directly precedes sleep. This is a body-scan guided relaxation practice with more evidence for nervous system downregulation than most relaxation methods. Free scripts available: search "Yoga Nidra for sleep." This is a tool, not a ritual — use only when needed.

As needed: Directly at bedtime
Protocol C

Racing Thoughts at Night — for founders whose minds stay active at bedtime, replay the day, or generate unresolved decisions and worries.

Target: Offload cognitive load before bed. Duration: 5 days to see effect.

1

Brain dump: 5-minute worry offload

Every night, 5 minutes before bed, write every open loop, worry, and tomorrow's priority into a notebook. The act of writing closes the loop in working memory — the brain no longer needs to rehearse it. This is not journaling for insight; it's a cognitive offload. Pen and paper only — no phone. Be ruthlessly fast and uncurated.

Daily: 5–7 min pre-sleep
2

Tomorrow's top 3 decisions: written

Write the 3 most important decisions or actions for tomorrow. The Zeigarnik effect — the brain's tendency to replay unfinished tasks — is directly reduced by committing them to an external system. This is the single most evidence-backed intervention for pre-sleep rumination in high-cognitive workers.

Immediately after brain dump
3

4-7-8 breathing for thought interrupt

If thoughts continue in bed: inhale 4 sec, hold 7 sec, exhale 8 sec — repeat 4–8 cycles. The extended exhale activates the vagus nerve and directly reduces sympathetic tone. The counting occupies working memory, interrupting the thought loop. This is a pattern break, not a meditation — it works even if you're skeptical.

In bed: As needed
4

If still awake after 20 min: leave the bed

Get up. Go to a different room. Low light, quiet activity (reading a physical book — not exciting), until sleepy. This is sleep restriction therapy principle: bed must be associated exclusively with sleep. Lying awake in bed teaches your nervous system that the bed is a place for alertness. Breaking this association is one of the most powerful long-term fixes for sleep-onset insomnia in high-performance populations.

As needed
Protocol D

Sleep Consistency & Schedule Repair — for founders with irregular schedules, social jet lag, or high variability in sleep/wake times.

Target: Stabilize the circadian rhythm. Duration: 21 days minimum. Most common profile in early-stage founders.

1

Identify your chronotype and design around it

Use the Munich Chronotype Questionnaire (MCTQ — free online) to identify whether you are a morning, intermediate, or evening type. Fighting your chronotype costs performance. If you are an evening type forced to wake at 6am, you are operating at a structural disadvantage. Design your schedule around your biology where possible — or use light therapy to shift your chronotype toward what your schedule demands.

One-time: Day 1
2

Morning bright light within 30 min of waking

10,000 lux light exposure (or outdoor natural light) within 30 minutes of waking, for 10–15 min. This is the primary signal that sets your circadian clock for the next 24 hours. It determines sleep onset timing at night (roughly 14–16h later). Skip this and your internal clock drifts. In Seoul's winter, a light therapy box is essential. This single step will improve both evening tiredness timing and morning alertness within 3–5 days.

Daily: Within 30 min of waking
3

Social jet lag protocol for weekends

Never sleep more than 1 hour past your weekday wake time on weekends. Social jet lag (large weekend sleep timing shifts) is equivalent to weekly transatlantic flights for your circadian system. You feel its effects Monday and Tuesday. If you need more sleep, go to bed earlier — do not wake later. This is the most commonly violated and impactful sleep rule in the founder population.

Weekly: Weekend anchor
4

Caffeine hard cutoff at 12–1pm

Caffeine has a 5–6 hour half-life — a coffee at 3pm means half its stimulant effect is still active at 8pm. This delays sleep onset and suppresses deep sleep even if you fall asleep easily. Set an alarm for your caffeine cutoff. Track it as a daily binary. Most founders significantly underestimate how much late caffeine costs them.

Daily: Hard cutoff
5

Build a consistent pre-sleep cue sequence

Same 3–4 actions in the same order every night, starting 45–60 min before bed. Example: dim lights → phone off → teeth → 5-min brain dump → read. The sequence itself becomes a conditioned sleep trigger over 2–3 weeks. Athletes use pre-performance rituals for the same reason — the sequence signals the nervous system what comes next. Design yours deliberately.

Daily: 45–60 min pre-sleep
